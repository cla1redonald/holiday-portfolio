# Devil's Advocate: Can a curated holiday portfolio platform generate enough booking volume and repeat usage to be a viable business?
Date: 2026-02-28

## Thesis

The "holiday portfolio" concept is an elegant intellectual construct that maps poorly onto how real people actually buy travel. The bull case rests on a cluster of assumptions — that frequent travellers plan annually and holistically, that the travel agent comeback signals demand for curation at scale, that commission economics work at niche volumes — each of which is shakier than it appears. The fatal flaw is not the idea but the structural trap: the platform captures planning value but bleeds booking conversions to incumbents with established trust and distribution advantages. Google's agentic AI travel push and Booking.com's AI Trip Planner are not future threats — they are present ones, built by companies with billions in acquisition budgets and zero need to reach minimum viable booking volume to survive. This platform is most likely to become another well-reviewed planning tool that users abandon at the moment that matters most: checkout.

## Evidence

### Evidence Point 1: The Desti Pattern — Planning and Booking Are Two Different Purchase Moments
- **Claim:** Travel planning products consistently fail to capture the booking conversion, because trust and inertia drive users back to OTAs at purchase time. This is a documented structural failure, not a fixable UX problem.
- **Source:** [PhocusWire — Why trip-planning startups struggle](https://www.phocuswire.com/trip-planning-startups-Phocuswright-Accenture); [Failory — 9 Failed Travel Startups](https://www.failory.com/startups/travel-failures)
- **Confidence:** 5 — This is explicitly named as the primary failure mode across multiple industry post-mortems. Desti, specifically cited in the bull case, is the canonical example: users found it valuable, then booked on Booking.com. Of ~700 travel planning companies tracked by Phocuswright, approximately 300 have failed, with inspiration/planning/booking platforms showing the highest shutdown rate.

### Evidence Point 2: "Portfolio Thinking" Is a Product Manager's Mental Model, Not a Consumer Behaviour
- **Claim:** The majority of travellers — including frequent ones — do not plan their year of travel as a unified budget exercise. They book trips reactively, spontaneously, and in response to deals. Imposing an annual "portfolio" framework onto this behaviour is a product assumption, not a validated need.
- **Source:** [StudyFinds — 2 in 3 people say unplanned trips are better than planned ones](https://studyfinds.org/spontaneous-trips-vacations/); [American Express Global Travel Trends 2025](https://www.americanexpress.com/en-gb/travel/discover/get-inspired/global-travel-trends-en-gb); [Psychology of Travel — Planning vs Spontaneous](https://psychologyoftravel.com/travel-planning-versus-spontaneous-travel-which-is-best/)
- **Confidence:** 4 — 65% of consumers do not plan their entire trip in advance; 77% of millennials and Gen Z have booked a last-minute trip. The target user (tech-savvy, 3+ trips/year) skews toward exactly the generation most likely to book spontaneously. The 16-hour planning pain stat reflects fragmented research across many trips, not a desire to do it all at once in a single portfolio session.

### Evidence Point 3: Google and Booking.com Are Not Future Threats — They Are Already Building This
- **Claim:** The "whitespace" framing ignores that the two largest incumbents in travel are actively deploying AI-powered curation and trip planning tools right now, with distribution that no startup can match.
- **Source:** [Skift — Google Is Building Agentic Travel Booking (Nov 2025)](https://skift.com/2025/11/17/google-is-building-agentic-travel-booking-plus-other-travel-ai-updates/); [Skift — Is Google Killing Online Travel?](https://skift.com/2024/05/24/is-google-killing-online-travel/); [Skift — Will Agentic AI Turn OTAs Into Passive Order Takers?](https://skift.com/2025/10/24/will-agentic-ai-turn-otas-into-passive-order-takers/)
- **Confidence:** 5 — Google's AI Mode is being built explicitly as a travel hub covering inspiration to booking completion. Booking.com's AI Trip Planner already has 41% of travellers expressing interest in using it. These are not feature flags — they are core strategic bets by companies spending $6.8B/year on marketing. A curated portfolio platform's moat (curation + personalisation) is precisely the capability both incumbents are building with LLMs at their disposal. Booking.com operates at 28M+ listed properties; the curation feature has essentially no incremental marginal cost for them.

### Evidence Point 4: Commission Economics Break Down at Niche Volume — The Maths Are Unforgiving
- **Claim:** Commission-based revenue at niche travel volumes cannot sustain the customer acquisition costs required to build a differentiated platform. The economics only work at LoveHolidays/Booking.com scale, not at startup scale.
- **Source:** [Cloudbeds — OTA Commission Guide 2026](https://www.cloudbeds.com/online-travel-agencies/commissions/); [Qubit Capital — Travel Market Unit Economics](https://qubit.capital/blog/travel-market-unit-economics-tam); [PhocusWire — Why you should never consider a travel planning startup](https://www.phocuswire.com/Why-you-should-never-consider-a-travel-planning-startup)
- **Confidence:** 4 — OTA commissions run 15-30% on accommodation, with OTAs themselves spending 20-25% of revenue on marketing to acquire customers. A niche platform earning 8-12% net commission on a £1,200 average holiday booking generates ~£120 per transaction. If CAC is even £80 (conservative — Booking.com's effective CAC per new customer is far higher), the LTV:CAC ratio requires 3+ repeat bookings per customer to break even. The 40% churn figure from the bear case — even if subscription-sourced — is directionally correct about retention difficulty. Secret Escapes, with £170M revenue and a famous brand, only produced £11.4M EBITDA in 2023. The margins are thin even at scale.

### Evidence Point 5: The Travel Agent Comeback Is a Niche Signal Being Read as a Market Signal
- **Claim:** The 26%→48% UK 18-24s travel agent usage statistic likely reflects a specific sub-behaviour (complex or luxury trip booking) being extrapolated into a broad market shift. The same demographic books spontaneously, uses AI tools, and is the primary driver of OTA growth.
- **Source:** [Condor Ferries — Gen Z Travel Statistics 2025](https://www.condorferries.co.uk/gen-z-travel-statistics); [WiT — Gen Y and Z travel intent for 2025](https://www.webintravel.com/gen-y-and-z-travel-intent-for-2025-social-media-a-major-deciding-factor-in-key-demographics/); [Travel Weekly — Gen Z and Millennials spearhead surge in AI travel planning](https://travelweekly.co.uk/news/gen-z-and-millennials-spearhead-surge-in-ai-travel-planning)
- **Confidence:** 3 — The survey data is genuinely mixed. 38% of Gen Z use travel agents, but the same cohort shows 80% preferring travel planning apps and social media. Gen Z's travel agent usage may reflect help with complex first-time international trips (gap years, multi-destination), not a sustained preference for curated annual portfolios. The "comeback" narrative suits travel industry trade press; the underlying data supports a more fragmented picture where AI tools are capturing the same convenience demand that agent usage supposedly signals.

## Risks to This Position

1. **The portfolio framing could be the distribution mechanism, not the literal product.** If "portfolio" is a UX metaphor for personalised trip recommendations delivered at the right moment — not a rigid annual budgeting exercise — then the behaviour mismatch concern diminishes. The question is whether the team has validated which interpretation users actually want.

2. **Incumbent neglect of the commission-sharing/loyalty angle is a real gap.** Google and Booking.com are building curation but not portfolio optimisation across budget, balance, or complementary trip types. A human-curated + algorithmic approach targeting repeat high-value bookers could carve a defensible niche precisely because incumbents are optimising for transaction volume, not customer portfolio outcomes. The Neilson 70% repeat rate suggests loyalty is achievable with the right curation.

3. **The Desti failure was a UX and trust problem, not necessarily a structural impossibility.** If this platform builds the booking layer natively — rather than redirecting to OTAs — the leakage problem is solved by design. LoveHolidays succeeded by owning the full funnel. The question is whether the team has the supplier relationships and capital to build it.

## Overall Confidence
**4** — The bear case here is strong because the planning-to-booking leakage problem is documented, persistent, and being actively worsened by incumbent AI investment; but it stops short of 5 because a narrow, booking-complete, high-LTV niche product aimed at frequent travellers is genuinely different from the broad planning tools that have failed.
