# Revenue Risks: The Bear Case for Holiday Portfolio
## A Devil's Advocate Research Report

**Date:** February 2026
**Purpose:** Honest assessment of why travel platform revenue models fail — with specific numbers, documented failures, and brutal implications for Holiday Portfolio.

---

## Executive Summary

The travel planning and personalisation space has a near-perfect failure rate for commission-dependent startups. This is not a matter of execution — it is a structural problem. The economics of travel commissions are thin, the incumbents are entrenched, booking leakage is endemic, and customers are loyal to the platforms they already trust with their credit cards. The bear case for Holiday Portfolio is not theoretical. It is the default outcome unless the model explicitly addresses each of these failure modes.

---

## Part 1: Why Travel Startups Fail Financially

### The Graveyard is Full

Failory's documented analysis of failed travel startups identifies a clear and repeating pattern: travel planning platforms attract users but fail to capture the economic value of the bookings those users make. The failure mode is structural, not operational.

**Documented failures:**

**Desti (2014)** — An AI-powered travel planner spun out of SRI International, the creators of Siri. Users found it useful. They found hotels, discovered destinations, made decisions. Then they went to Booking.com or the hotel's own website to actually book. Co-founder Nadav Gur's post-mortem was explicit: "They will go to the channel they are used to." The core product worked. The revenue model did not. Seven of nine employees joined Nokia. The company generated near-zero booking revenue before closing.
- Source: [Failory](https://www.failory.com/cemetery/desti), [Skift](https://skift.com/2014/06/26/lessons-from-the-demise-of-another-failed-travel-startup/)

**Culture Trip (2024)** — Raised $155 million. Pivoted three times: content platform → hotel booking OTA → curated group tours. None of the pivots found sustainable margins. Despite significant investment and a content audience, the company was unable to convert editorial engagement into booking revenue at scale. Sold to U.S. News & World Report in February 2024 for an undisclosed sum. The sale price was widely understood to be significantly below the $155M raised.
- Source: [Skift](https://skift.com/2024/02/29/culture-trips-final-pivot-sells-to-u-s-news-after-raising-155-million/)

**Triposo** — Acquired by Musement (a TUI subsidiary) in 2016 after raising approximately $9M. Could not build sustainable revenue from travel guide content and affiliate commissions. Absorbed for its technology, not its business.
- Source: [PhocusWire](https://www.phocuswire.com/Triposo-raises-again-with-3-1M-for-its-travel-guide-apps)

**Hitlist** — Flight deals app. TIME's Best App of 2016. Last updated in 2018. No formal shutdown announcement; the app simply went dark. Tried a subscription model at $4.99/month but could not retain paying users in a market where Going.com and Google Flights were free.
- Source: [Hayley Bird / The Travel App That Ghosted Me](https://www.hayleybird.com/blog/what-happened-to-hitlist)

**Utrip** — AI itinerary personalisation. "The cost of keeping the recommendation engine alive was too high." Revenue from affiliate commissions could not fund the ML infrastructure needed to maintain the product's core value proposition.
- Source: [Failory](https://www.failory.com/startups/travel-failures)

**Onepagetrip** — Itinerary sharing community. Co-founder post-mortem: "Not having a plan to make money from the beginning was a stupid rookie mistake." The company spent two years building a product with no monetisation path.
- Source: [Failory](https://www.failory.com/startups/travel-failures)

**Google Trips (killed August 2019)** — Google built a fully-featured travel planning app with itinerary management, offline maps, and reservation aggregation. It had Google's distribution, brand, and data. They shut it down anyway — not because it failed, but because Google concluded the features were better served inside Maps and Search. The lesson: even Google could not build a standalone monetisable travel planning app. Features get absorbed by platforms with booking surfaces. Planning tools without booking surfaces are utilities, not businesses.
- Source: [Failory/Google Trips](https://www.failory.com/google/trips), [Engadget](https://www.engadget.com/2019-08-05-google-shuts-down-trips-app.html)

**TripIt (20M users, near-zero booking revenue)** — TripIt is instructive precisely because it succeeded as a product while failing as a commission business. Its ad-based model was "basically breakeven" even with 10 million users. The platform aggregated itineraries but did not control the booking moment. Airlines blocked automatic frequent flyer tracking (Southwest, Delta, United, American) when they deemed TripIt was harvesting what they considered proprietary intellectual property. The company monetised through TripIt Pro subscriptions (~$49/yr) and corporate B2B, not through bookings. It was acquired by Concur (SAP) in 2011. Booking commissions were never material.
- Source: [Liviu Tudor blog](https://liviutudor.com/2014/06/21/of-tripit-and-their-business-model/), [Skift](https://skift.com/2013/05/07/tripit-close-to-launching-seat-tracker-tool-but-are-best-days-in-the-past/)

**Secret Escapes (thin margins despite £104M revenue)** — Secret Escapes reported £104.1M underlying revenue in 2022, with gross bookings value of £439.6M. Operating profit was £1.6M in 2023 — achieved only after a £14.8M operating loss the year prior. Pre-tax loss in 2023 was still £7.1M despite revenue of £170M. On £523.8M gross bookings, they kept approximately 20p in the pound as revenue, then spent almost all of it on marketing and operations. This is the best-case scenario for a UK luxury travel flash-sale model with 20M members, £100M in revenue, and a decade of operating history: near-zero net profit.
- Source: [Travel Weekly](https://travelweekly.co.uk/news/secret-escapes-group-recovers-to-operating-profit), [City A.M.](https://www.cityam.com/secret-escapes-post-pandemic-demand-helps-significantly-cut-loss/)

### The Structural Pattern

PhocusWire's documented analysis of travel planning startups identifies the same conclusion reached independently by multiple operators: "The most common bad startup idea in 2012 was travel planning software. This is still true in 2024." The reason given is not market size — it is the commission economics and the booking leakage problem. Users plan on your platform and book on someone else's.

Travel startup funding dipped to $5.3B in 2023 and $5.8B in 2024 — both 10-year lows. Q1 2025 saw under $1B raised, a 40% drop from Q1 2024. Investors have seen this failure pattern repeatedly and have reduced allocation to the category.
- Source: [Phocuswright](https://www.phocuswright.com/Travel-Research/Research-Updates/2025/travel-startup-funding-falters-in-q1-2025)

## Part 2: The Real Margin Problem

### What Commission Rates Actually Are (Not the Advertised Version)

The travel industry publishes commission rates that look attractive. The actual economics are materially different.

**Booking.com affiliate program (standard):**
- Advertised: commission on bookings
- Actual rate: **4% of the accommodation booking value** for standard affiliates
- Example: £200 hotel booking × 4% = **£8 earned**
- This is not 4% of what Booking.com earns. This is 4% of the total booking value.
- Source: [Booking.com Affiliate Support Centre](https://affiliates.support.booking.com/kb/s/article/Commission-and-Payments), [Reacheffect](https://reacheffect.com/blog/how-much-does-booking-com-pay-affiliates/)

**Travel Agent Affiliate Program (TAAP — for volume players):**
- Tiered: 25% of Booking.com's commission (not of the booking value) for 0–50 bookings/month
- Booking.com charges hotels 15–25%. At 20% commission on a £200 booking, Booking.com earns £40. TAAP affiliate earns 25% of £40 = **£10 per booking**.
- To reach 40% tier requires 500+ bookings per month — which no early-stage startup achieves.
- Source: [Mize Tech](https://mize.tech/blog/all-about-the-booking-com-affiliate-partner-program-for-travel-agents/)

**The actual math for Holiday Portfolio (Year 1):**
A user books a £1,200 UK city break (2 nights, family of 4). Assuming:
- Hotel commission via affiliate: 4% × £800 room cost = £32
- Any flights booked via Duffel or similar: £2 per booking flat fee minimum
- Net commission per transaction: **£30–£40**

This is the ceiling for a typical mid-range booking. Premium packages do better. Budget bookings do worse.

**What happens to that £30–£40:**

| Cost Item | Estimated Amount |
|---|---|
| Payment processing (high-risk, 3–4% of transaction) | £36–£48 |
| ATOL contribution per passenger (£2.50 × 4 pax) | £10 |
| Customer support (£6.26/call average, 30% of bookings need support) | £1.88 |
| Fraud/chargeback reserve (0.89% rate × £2.40 per £1 lost) | variable |
| **Gross margin per booking** | **Often negative on low-value transactions** |

Sources: [CAA Small Business ATOL](https://www.caa.co.uk/atol-protection/trade/about-atol/small-business-atol/), [PayCompass chargebacks](https://paycompass.com/blog/travel-industry-chargebacks/), [Neural Voice UK travel costs](https://www.neural-voice.ai/blog/how-to-save-money-travel-contact-centers-ai-voice-cost-reduction)

### Payment Processing: Travel is High-Risk by Default

Travel businesses are classified as high-risk by card networks. This is not negotiable for a new entrant.

- Standard merchant account rate: 1.5–2.9%
- High-risk travel merchant account rate: **3.5–6%+ per transaction**
- Visa annual high-risk registration fee: **£950 per acquiring provider** (increased from £500 in April 2024)
- Mastercard registration: £500/year
- Rolling reserve: **5–15% of credit card deposits** held for 6–12 months (working capital impact)
- Chargeback fee: £20–£50 per dispute
- Source: [TailoredPay](https://tailoredpay.com/blog/high-risk-merchant-account-fees/), [Vendoservices](https://vendoservices.com/blog/visa-mastercard-high-risk-fees-and-virp/)

The rolling reserve alone is a significant cash flow constraint for a startup. If Holiday Portfolio processes £100,000 in bookings in Month 3, the processor holds £10,000–£15,000 for six months. This is capital that is unavailable for operations.

### The Chargeback Problem

Travel has **one of the highest chargeback rates of any ecommerce category**:
- Travel sector chargeback rate: **0.89%** (vs ecommerce average ~0.6%)
- Between 2023 and 2024: chargeback rates in travel climbed **816%** (from 0.1% to 0.916%)
- Average chargeback cost: **£2.40 for every £1 of disputed booking value**
- Average chargeback value in travel: **$120 (approximately £95)**
- Visa threshold for high-risk designation: 0.65%. Travel routinely exceeds this.

The causes: advance bookings, service-not-as-described, cancellation policy disputes, fraud targeting travel merchants (who hold payment long before delivery).

If Holiday Portfolio processes 500 bookings in Year 1 at average £800 value: expect 4–5 chargebacks, at £2.40 cost per £1, meaning approximately £960–£1,150 in chargeback-related losses before any processor fees.
- Source: [Chargeflow](https://www.chargeflow.io/blog/chargeback-statistics-trends-costs-solutions), [PayCompass Travel Chargebacks](https://paycompass.com/blog/travel-industry-chargebacks/)

### ATOL Compliance: The Unavoidable Cost

If Holiday Portfolio books packages (flight + hotel), ATOL protection is a legal requirement in the UK, not optional.

- **ATOL Protection Contribution (APC): £2.50 per passenger per booking**
- Small Business ATOL application fee: £1,223 (upfront)
- Advance APC payment: £1,250 (upfront)
- Initial compliance cost before booking a single customer: **£2,473**
- Per family-of-4 booking: £10 directly off margin

This cost applies to all protected bookings. It cannot be avoided without restricting the product to accommodation-only, which eliminates the holiday portfolio value proposition.
- Source: [UK CAA](https://www.caa.co.uk/atol-protection/trade/about-atol/small-business-atol/)

### Customer Acquisition Cost: The Real Number

The travel industry has seen CAC increase ~35% from 2022 to 2025. Estimates for UK travel platforms:

- OTA CAC benchmark: **£15–£45** (established platforms with brand recognition)
- Hotels/airlines CAC: £50–£200
- A new travel startup with no brand recognition: likely **£80–£200+ per acquired customer** via paid channels
- 77% of travel marketers unknowingly target their own existing customers through paid media (money burned on retention, not acquisition)
- Source: [PhocusWire rising CAC](https://www.phocuswire.com/addressing-rising-customer-acquisition-costs-travel), [Hotel News Resource](https://www.hotelnewsresource.com/article137786.html)

If Holiday Portfolio spends £100 acquiring a customer who generates £30–£40 in commission on their first booking: the payback period is 2.5–3 bookings minimum, assuming zero churn and perfect retention. In travel, where the hospitality industry has only a **55% customer retention rate**, this is optimistic.

### The Booking.com/Expedia Margin Reality

The two largest OTAs — companies with global brand recognition, billions in marketing spend, and decades of optimisation — are facing margin compression:

- Booking Holdings EBITDA margin: **36.6% trailing twelve months** (down from 39% in 2019)
- Global EBITDA across travel industry: **15% below pre-pandemic levels** despite volume recovery to 1,465M international arrivals
- OTAs maintain ~40% market share at pre-COVID levels but profitability has not recovered proportionally

If Booking.com with $166B in gross bookings and decades of optimisation is seeing margin decline, a startup with thousands of bookings is not going to find better unit economics.
- Source: [Mize Tech high volume low margin](https://mize.tech/blog/2025-travel-industry-recovery-high-volume-low-margin/), Booking Holdings 2024 annual filings

## Part 3: Fintech Revenue Risks

### The Price Freeze Problem

Hopper's fintech model is the most-cited example of travel fintech monetisation. It deserves honest scrutiny.

**What Hopper says:** Price Freeze generates significant revenue and drives 40% of annual bookings value.

**What Hopper doesn't say publicly:** The company is not profitable. Despite $850M in revenue in 2024 (up 21.4% YoY) and $740M in VC funding, Hopper has never disclosed positive net income. At a $5B valuation with $850M revenue, the company is priced as a growth asset, not a profitable business. The fintech products generate revenue but require Hopper to absorb the risk of price movements — and that risk is material.

**The class action:** A class action lawsuit alleged Hopper's Price Freeze misled consumers by advertising "full protection" against price increases while hiding a $100 service cap in a confirmation email — only visible after purchase by clicking a circled "i" icon. When flight prices rose by hundreds of dollars, customers were personally liable for all but $100. The case was voluntarily dismissed in March 2023 (possibly settled), but the consumer complaints on TripAdvisor and the FTC remain active.

**The actuarial risk:** Price Freeze is essentially a short-term options contract on flight prices. If prices go up more than expected (which happened across the industry in 2022–2024 due to demand surges and capacity constraints), the product issuer absorbs the loss — or caps coverage and angers customers. Neither outcome is good for a startup without actuarial infrastructure.

**For Holiday Portfolio:** Offering any form of price guarantee requires either:
1. FCA authorisation as an insurance intermediary (6–12 month process, costly)
2. A licensed insurance partner who takes the risk (reducing margin dramatically)
3. Strict caps that create customer dissatisfaction (the Hopper problem)

Sources: [McKinsey/Hopper](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/travel-logistics-and-infrastructure/our-insights/travel-disruptors-bringing-fintech-to-travel-booking), [Class Action Hopper](https://www.classaction.org/news/hoppers-price-freeze-protection-does-not-fully-protect-travelers-from-ticket-price-increases-class-action-says), [Top Class Actions](https://topclassactions.com/lawsuit-settlements/travel/hopper-class-action-lawsuit-alleges-apps-price-freeze-feature-misleading/)

### FCA Regulation: The Blocker for Travel Fintech in the UK

Selling or arranging insurance in the UK — including travel insurance, price protection products, or cancel-for-any-reason coverage — requires FCA authorisation unless covered by a narrow exemption.

- Standard FCA authorisation timeline: **6–12 months**
- New interim regime: up to **18 months** before full authorisation
- Any "price freeze" or "cancel for any reason" product offered by Holiday Portfolio would likely constitute insurance distribution requiring authorisation
- Operating without authorisation is a criminal offence under FSMA 2000

This means Holiday Portfolio cannot launch travel fintech products at MVP stage. They require either:
- Full FCA authorisation before launch (6–18 months, significant legal costs)
- A fully authorised insurance partner as the principal (commercially complex, margin dilutive)

Sources: [FCA Insurance sellers](https://www.fca.org.uk/firms/authorisation/process/insurance-sellers), [Sprintlaw FCA guide](https://sprintlaw.co.uk/articles/who-needs-to-be-fca-regulated-in-the-uk/)

### What Happens During Disruption Events

Travel platforms face existential risk during industry disruption events:

- **2024 disruptions:** 78% of business travellers experienced disruptions. 27% faced cancellations. The July 2024 CrowdStrike IT outage disrupted global aviation for days. Only 57% of flights operated without delay.
- **Pandemic:** Revenue went to zero for 12–18 months. Commission-based businesses with no alternative income had zero revenue against fixed costs.
- **Volcanic disruption (Eyjafjallajokull 2010):** 100,000+ flights cancelled. Travel operators absorbed costs.
- **Strikes:** UK and German travellers hit hardest in 2024 (27–29% disruption rate)

A commission-based travel platform has no revenue during disruption periods but often faces increased costs (customer support, refund management, dispute resolution). The model is asymmetrically vulnerable to black swan events.

Sources: [GlobeNewswire TravelPerk 2024](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2024/11/21/2985272/0/en/Another-Turbulent-Year-for-Business-Travelers-Nearly-8-in-10-experienced-travel-disruptions-in-2024.html)

## Part 4: Subscription Model Reality

### Going.com (Scott's Cheap Flights): The Best-Case Example

Going.com has 2M+ subscribers and represents the most successful travel subscription model outside of loyalty programs. Key facts:

- Pricing: Limited (free) / Premium ($49/yr) / Elite ($149/yr)
- The model works because the value is immediate and tangible: users receive flight deal alerts to their inbox. The subscription is for information, not for booking.
- Going.com does not primarily earn commission on bookings — it earns subscription revenue. Users are sent to Google Flights or airlines directly to book.
- This is the critical distinction: **Going.com is not a commission business. It is a subscription information business that deliberately avoids the booking layer.**
- Source: [Going.com](https://www.going.com/how-it-works), [Indie Hackers](https://www.indiehackers.com/interview/scotts-cheap-flights-from-small-side-project-to-booming-business-de62ca54b1)

**No specific churn rate has been published.** Industry benchmarks for consumer travel subscriptions suggest:
- Travel and hospitality customer retention rate: **55%** (one of the lowest across subscription categories)
- This implies annual churn of approximately 45% — meaning nearly half of subscribers do not renew
- General subscription business churn benchmark: 4.2%/month average (travel is likely higher)

Sources: [Recurly churn benchmarks](https://recurly.com/research/churn-rate-benchmarks/), [BillingPlatform churn by industry](https://billingplatform.com/blog/average-churn-rate-by-industry/)

### The Subscription vs Commission Tension

Offering both subscription and commission revenue creates a structural tension:

1. **Subscription logic:** Users pay because they trust the platform. The trust is built on unbiased recommendations.
2. **Commission logic:** Revenue comes from bookings made through affiliate links. This creates an incentive to recommend partners who pay more commission, not necessarily the best option for the user.
3. If users perceive the platform as commercially motivated in recommendations, they downgrade or cancel subscriptions.
4. If the platform genuinely optimises for user outcomes, commission revenue is lower (it recommends the best value option, which may be direct with the hotel).

There is no clean resolution. The platforms that have attempted both (Kayak, Trivago) operate at scale where neither concern matters much individually. At startup scale, the tension is existential for trust.

**Free-to-paid conversion in travel subscriptions:** No reliable public data exists for niche travel subscription services. Going.com's Indie Hackers interview suggests conversion improved "once the product was good enough that word of mouth spread" — a circular description that provides no baseline metric.

Sources: [Purchasely travel subscriptions](https://www.purchasely.com/blog/travel-industry-subscriptions-everywhere-is-a-growing-trend), [RevenueCat State of Subscription Apps 2024](https://www.revenuecat.com/state-of-subscription-apps-2024/)

## Part 5: Honest Revenue Assessment for Holiday Portfolio

### The Booking Leakage Problem

The single biggest threat to Holiday Portfolio's commission model is the behaviour that killed Desti, limited TripIt, and constrained every travel planning platform: **users plan on your platform and book on someone else's.**

This is not a UX problem. It is a trust and habit problem:
- Customers have credit card details saved on Booking.com
- They trust Booking.com's cancellation policies
- They know Booking.com's customer service number
- Booking.com has their payment history and preferences
- Booking.com has the same inventory at the same price

"Even when there's a 'Book Now' button on a travel planning platform, if customers can buy the same thing at the same price on a channel they've done business with before — why wouldn't they?" This is the core finding from multiple post-mortems.

61% of business travellers book outside managed channels even when those channels are employer-mandated. For leisure travellers with no mandate, the leakage rate is likely higher.

Sources: [Fox World Travel leakage](https://www.foxworldtravel.com/business-travel-blog/what-is-booking-leakage-and-how-does-it-affect-your-travel-program/), [Grasp Tech leakage](https://www.grasptech.com/blog/the-5-biggest-sources-of-travel-program-leakage-and-how-to-stop-them)

### Revenue Streams: Achievable vs Aspirational

| Revenue Stream | Status | Reality |
|---|---|---|
| Affiliate hotel commissions (Booking.com) | Achievable | 4% of booking value, ~£8–£32 per transaction, requires traffic volume |
| Affiliate flight commissions | Achievable | £2/booking flat fee (Duffel) or 2–4% via GDS, very thin |
| Tour/experience affiliate | Achievable | 8–15% on activities, better margins but lower booking values |
| Subscription (premium recommendations) | Aspirational | Requires proven value before users pay; churn risk is high |
| Price freeze / travel fintech | Aspirational | Blocked by FCA requirements; requires insurance authorisation |
| B2B corporate travel / concierge | Aspirational | Longer sales cycle, different product entirely |
| Advertising / sponsored placements | Achievable | Destroys trust if implemented early; scale needed for meaningful revenue |

### Realistic Timeline to First Pound of Revenue

**Month 1–3:** No revenue. Building product, acquiring affiliate accounts, onboarding initial users.

**Month 4–6:** First affiliate commissions possible. Expect 30–50 bookings generated at £20–£40 each = **£600–£2,000** gross. After payment processing costs, chargeback reserve, and any ATOL contributions: **£300–£800 net**.

**Month 7–12:** Growth with heavy marketing spend. If CAC is £100 and you acquire 500 users, marketing spend is £50,000. If 20% convert to booking (generous) × £30 average commission = **£3,000** in Year 1 commission revenue from those 500 users. The £50,000 marketing spend is not covered.

**Year 1 Revenue (realistic bear case):**
- Total commission revenue: £5,000–£20,000
- Total cost base (product + marketing + compliance + legal): £150,000–£300,000
- **Year 1 loss: £130,000–£280,000**

**Year 2 Revenue (realistic bear case):**
- Assuming 3× booking volume, better conversion, some repeat customers
- Total commission revenue: £25,000–£60,000
- Cost base (team + marketing): £200,000–£400,000
- **Year 2 loss: £140,000–£340,000**

**Year 3 (breakeven scenario, if it exists):**
- Requires 10,000+ bookings per year at £30–£40 each = £300,000–£400,000 gross commission
- After all costs: possible breakeven IF marketing spend is controlled and the team is small
- Most likely outcome: not yet reached by Year 3 without venture funding

### What Would Kill the Revenue Model

Ranked by probability:

1. **Booking leakage:** Users use Holiday Portfolio for inspiration, book on Booking.com. This is the #1 failure mode. (Very High Probability)

2. **CAC exceeds LTV permanently:** The cost to acquire a customer is £100–£200. The first booking generates £30–£40. If repeat booking rate is low (which it is in travel — people book 1–2 holidays per year), payback never happens. (High Probability in Year 1)

3. **Affiliate programme changes:** Booking.com, Expedia, or other partners change commission terms unilaterally. Both have done this historically. Holiday Portfolio has no contractual protection. (Medium-High Probability)

4. **OTA disintermediation:** Booking.com invests in personalisation features and removes the differentiation that Holiday Portfolio offers. Google Travel already does personalised recommendations. (High Probability, 18–36 month horizon)

5. **Disruption event:** A pandemic-scale, or even a moderate disruption event (airline strikes, Iceland volcano, major geopolitical event) eliminates bookings for 3–6 months while fixed costs continue. (Low-Medium Probability per year, but near-certain over a 5-year horizon)

6. **FCA enforcement on fintech products:** If Holiday Portfolio launches any price protection or cancel-for-any-reason feature without proper FCA authorisation, it faces enforcement action, fines, and reputational damage. (Probability depends entirely on product decisions)

7. **Chargeback rate triggers high-risk merchant exit:** If chargeback rate exceeds 1%, card processors can terminate the merchant account. Travel already sits at 0.89%. One major fraud incident or disruption event could push Holiday Portfolio above threshold. (Medium Probability)

## Summary of the Bear Case

The bear case for Holiday Portfolio is not a pessimistic scenario. It is the **median outcome** for a travel planning startup with a commission-based revenue model, based on 20+ years of documented failures:

- Year 1 revenue is measured in thousands, not hundreds of thousands
- Every meaningful commission transaction has 3–5 cost layers that erode margin to near-zero
- Booking leakage is structural, not fixable with better UX alone
- The platforms users trust with their credit cards (Booking.com, Expedia) are also the affiliate partners Holiday Portfolio depends on — creating a permanent conflict of interest
- Subscription revenue cannot begin until users trust the platform, which requires time and money to build
- Travel fintech (price freeze, CFAR insurance) requires FCA authorisation before launch in the UK
- The travel industry's best-capitalised and most-experienced operators are earning EBITDA margins 15% below pre-pandemic levels in 2025
- Even Secret Escapes, with 20M members and £170M revenue, generated only £1.6M operating profit in its best recent year

Holiday Portfolio's differentiation — personalisation based on portfolio-based preference discovery — is a genuine product insight. But it does not change the distribution economics, the booking leakage behaviour, or the FCA regulatory environment.

The question is not whether the model can work in theory. The question is whether Holiday Portfolio can reach sufficient booking volume to sustain a business before running out of capital. Based on documented precedent, most travel planning startups do not.
